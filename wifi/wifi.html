What is Wi-Fi?

Wi-Fi is a wireless networking technology that allows devices to connect to the internet and communicate with each other. It operates over radio frequencies and provides high-speed internet access without the need for physical cables.

Wi-Fi 4 (802.11n)

Wi-Fi 4 (802.11n) is an older standard, but it still offers decent performance for basic internet usage. It operates in both 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz bands and provides speeds up to 600 Mbps. This standard is widely used in older devices.

  • Operates on 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz
  • Speeds up to 600 Mbps
  • Good range, but lower speeds compared to newer standards

Wi-Fi 5 (802.11ac)

Wi-Fi 5 (802.11ac) is the standard used by most modern devices. It operates primarily on the 5 GHz band, providing faster speeds, higher capacity, and less interference. Wi-Fi 5 can deliver speeds up to 3.5 Gbps in ideal conditions.

  • Operates mostly on 5 GHz
  • Speeds up to 3.5 Gbps
  • Improved performance, especially in crowded environments

Wi-Fi 6 (802.11ax)

Wi-Fi 6 is the latest Wi-Fi standard that provides faster speeds, better efficiency, and improved performance in crowded areas. It operates on both 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz bands, with speeds up to 9.6 Gbps. Wi-Fi 6 also introduces better support for IoT devices and reduced latency.

  • Operates on both 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz
  • Speeds up to 9.6 Gbps
  • Better efficiency, less congestion, and improved battery life for devices

Why Does Wi-Fi 6 Matter?

Wi-Fi 6 brings significant improvements over older standards, especially for environments with many connected devices. It provides faster speeds, lower latency, and better overall performance, making it essential for modern homes and businesses.
